Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia/Lymphoma (ALL)

Clinical Presentation

  • Bone/joint pain (expanding blast population)
  • Fatigue, pallor (anemia)
  • Petechiae, easy bruising (thrombocytopenia)
  • Fever (infection or cytokines)
  • T-cell ALL: anterior mediastinal mass

Pathology/Morphology

  • Lymphoblasts with large nucleoli, fine chromatin
  • Small amount of bluish cytoplasm
  • NO Auer rods (distinguishes from AML)
  • TdT positive (terminal deoxynucleotidyl transferase)

Lab Findings

  • WBC variable (may be low, normal, or high)
  • ‚â•20% blasts in blood or bone marrow
  • Anemia and thrombocytopenia
  • B-cell: CD19, CD22, CD79a positive
  • T-cell: CD2, CD3, CD4, CD7, CD8 positive

Treatment

  • Induction: Vincristine + Daunorubicin + L-asparaginase + Glucocorticoids
  • Consolidation and maintenance therapy
  • Intrathecal chemo (methotrexate, cytarabine, hydrocortisone) for CNS
  • TKI (dasatinib) for Philadelphia chromosome positive

Risk Factors/Epidemiology

  • Most common childhood cancer (25% of all)
  • Down syndrome (most common leukemia in Down syndrome)
  • t(9;22) Philadelphia chromosome (20% adults, 3-5% children)
  • Good prognosis in children, poor in adults

Benign Mature Neutrophilia

Clinical Presentation

  • Fever and signs of bacterial infection
  • May have inflammation symptoms

Pathology/Morphology

  • Toxic granulation (increased primary granules)
  • D√∂hle bodies (blue-gray cytoplasmic inclusions)
  • Cytoplasmic vacuoles (seen in sepsis)
  • All neutrophils are mature (2-5 nuclear lobes)

Lab Findings

  • Neutrophils >7700/mm¬≥
  • Normal mature segmented neutrophils

Treatment

  • Treat underlying bacterial infection or inflammation

Risk Factors

  • Bacterial infection (most common)
  • Inflammation
  • Stress, smoking, corticosteroids, G-CSF

Leukemoid Reaction (Benign Immature Neutrophilia)

Clinical Presentation

  • Signs of severe infection or inflammation

Pathology/Morphology

  • Left shift (bands, metamyelocytes, myelocytes present)
  • Immature neutrophil precursors in blood
  • May have toxic changes

Lab Findings

  • Increased mature AND immature neutrophils
  • WBC returns to normal after treatment

Treatment

  • Treat underlying infection or disease

Risk Factors

  • Severe infection or inflammatory disease
  • SLE, rheumatoid arthritis
  • Chemotherapy drugs (G-CSF)

Leukoerythroblastic Reaction

Clinical Presentation

  • Pallor and fatigue (anemia symptoms)

Pathology/Morphology

  • Immature neutrophils (myelocytes) present
  • Nucleated RBCs (immature erythrocytes) present
  • Anisocytosis, poikilocytosis, polychromasia

Lab Findings

  • Both immature myeloid cells and nucleated RBCs
  • Signs of anemia

Treatment

  • Address underlying severe anemia
  • Investigate for bone marrow infiltration

Risk Factors

  • Severe anemia (high marrow output)
  • Bone marrow infiltration by neoplasm or fibrosis

Benign Lymphocytosis

Clinical Presentation

  • May be asymptomatic
  • Viral symptoms: fever, lymphadenopathy, jaundice

Pathology/Morphology

  • Reactive lymphocytes (larger, basophilic cytoplasm)
  • Downey type II cells (EBV - "fried egg" appearance)
  • Lymphocytes may be bilobed or have azurophilic granules

Lab Findings

  • Lymphocytes >4000/mm¬≥
  • Can reach up to 100,000/mm¬≥ in infectious lymphocytosis

Treatment

  • Supportive care for viral infection

Risk Factors

  • Viral infections: EBV (mono), CMV, HIV, hepatitis, COVID-19
  • Bordetella pertussis (whooping cough)
  • DRESS syndrome (drug reaction)

Benign Lymphadenopathy

Clinical Presentation

  • Soft, moveable, tender enlarged nodes (>1cm)
  • May have fever, viral symptoms
  • Location gives clues to underlying cause

Pathology/Morphology

  • Follicular hyperplasia (B-cell response, germinal centers)
  • Paracortical hyperplasia (T-cell response, expanded paracortex)
  • Tingible body macrophages in germinal centers

Lab Findings

  • May have elevated WBC
  • Positive viral testing (HIV, EBV, etc.)

Treatment

  • Treat underlying infection (antibiotics/antivirals)
  • Stop offending drugs
  • Anti-inflammatory meds for autoimmune disease

Risk Factors

  • Viral infections (HIV, CMV, EBV, measles, mumps)
  • Bacterial infections (TB, typhoid, brucellosis)
  • Autoimmune diseases (RA, SLE, Sj√∂gren syndrome)
  • Drugs (phenytoin, antibiotics, antiseizure meds)
